Nobody Labeled the Internet: How Foundation Models Actually Learn

Why one training trick, self-supervision, turned language models into the general-purpose engines behind ChatGPT, Copilot, and basically every AI app you've touched this year, and why "AI engineering" became its own job.

The big idea

Foundation models feel like magic because they can write, code, and chat about almost anything without being built for any one of those jobs specifically. The trick behind that generality is boring on the surface: these models are trained to predict the next chunk of text over and over, on an enormous pile of ordinary writing. Because the "right answer" for that guessing game is just whatever word already comes next in the text, nobody has to sit down and label the data by hand. That one shift (learning without needing human-labeled examples) is what let these models scale up to sizes nobody could previously afford to train, and that scale is what turned "building an app with a model" into a discipline of its own: AI engineering.

Key terms

  • Language modelA system that has learned the statistical patterns of a language well enough to predict how likely a word is to show up in a given context. Example: given "My favorite color is __", a good English language model should rate "blue" as far more likely than "car".
  • TokenThe basic unit a language model reads and writes. It might be a whole word, part of a word, or a character, depending on the model. For GPT-4 specifically, the source notes an average token is roughly three-quarters of a word, so 100 tokens is about 75 words.
  • Tokenization / vocabularyTokenization is the process of chopping raw text into tokens. The vocabulary is the fixed list of tokens a given model knows. Small vocabularies (tens of thousands of tokens) get combined to represent huge numbers of words, the same way a small alphabet builds huge numbers of words.
  • Autoregressive language modelPredicts the next token using only what came before it. This is the type behind almost all of today's text generation (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.), because you can keep feeding its own output back in to keep generating, one token at a time.
  • Masked language modelPredicts missing tokens using context from both before and after the gap ("fill in the blank"). Good for understanding tasks like classification, less suited to open-ended generation. BERT is the well-known example named in the source.
  • Generative AIThe label for models that produce open-ended output from a fixed, finite vocabulary, rather than picking from a small fixed set of answers (like a spam/not-spam classifier would).
  • Supervision (supervised learning)Training a model on examples that a human has explicitly labeled, e.g. transactions marked "fraud" or "not fraud".
  • Self-supervisionTraining where the "label" is inferred straight from the input itself, no human needed. This is exactly what makes next-token prediction work: the next word in a real sentence is the label.
  • ParameterA number inside a model that gets adjusted during training. Model size is usually measured in parameter count. What counts as "large" keeps moving: 117 million parameters was considered large for GPT in 2018; by the time this material was written, 100 billion was the bar.
  • Multimodal modelA model that can take in and/or generate more than one type of data, not just text (also images, sometimes audio, video, and more).
  • Foundation modelA large, general-purpose model (language-only or multimodal) that's powerful and general enough to be "built upon" for many different downstream applications, rather than trained for one narrow task.
  • EmbeddingA vector (a list of numbers) meant to capture the meaning of a piece of data (text, an image, etc.) so that similar things end up with similar vectors. Mentioned here as the output of models like CLIP; a fuller explanation is flagged as coming later.
  • Prompt engineering, RAG, finetuningThree different ways to adapt a general-purpose foundation model to a specific task: crafting better instructions (prompt engineering), giving it a database to pull facts from (retrieval-augmented generation, RAG), or further training it on task-specific examples (finetuning).
  • AI engineeringThe practice of building applications on top of already-existing foundation models, as opposed to training a model from scratch (which is closer to what "ML engineering" traditionally meant).
  • Model as a serviceThe pattern where a handful of well-resourced organizations train big models and expose them to everyone else through an API, so you can use a powerful model without hosting or training one yourself.

How it works

Here's the grounding piece: a "foundation model" is really just a very large, very general language (or multimodal) model that was trained cheaply enough, on enough data, to become useful for far more than the one task it was technically trained on. It matters right now because that cheapness came from removing a very old bottleneck in machine learning: the need for humans to label training data by hand. Once that bottleneck was gone, models could be trained on basically all the text on the internet, and once a model has absorbed that much of how language and ideas fit together, it turns out you can point it at almost any task and get something usable. That's the whole chain reaction this material walks through, and it's worth taking slowly because every later idea in the field builds on it.

1. Language models started as a much narrower idea: predicting what comes next

A language model, in the plainest sense, is a system that has learned how likely different words are to appear in a given context. This isn't new: Sherlock Holmes decoded a cipher using letter frequency in an 1905 story, and Claude Shannon formalized language statistics in a 1951 paper on entropy, work that still underpins language modeling today.

Two flavors of language model matter here, and they differ in which direction they're allowed to "look":

  • Autoregressive models predict the next token using only what came before ("My favorite color is __"). Because you can feed a model's own prediction back in as new input, this lets a model generate text one token at a time, indefinitely. This is the type behind almost all generation today, and it's what the rest of this digest (and this book) means by "language model" unless stated otherwise.
  • Masked models predict a gap using context from both sides ("My favorite __ is blue"). BERT is the canonical example. These are better suited to understanding tasks (classification, spotting errors in code) than to open-ended writing, and are less commonly used for generation now.
Autoregressive models only ever look backward; masked models get to peek both ways. This is my own simplified sketch of the idea, not a reproduction of a figure from the source.

2. Tokens sit deliberately between letters and whole words

A model doesn't read letters or whole words directly, it reads tokens, which can be a whole word, a fragment (like -tion), or a character, depending on how the model's creators set it up. GPT-4's vocabulary has 100,256 possible tokens; Mixtral 8x7B's has 32,000. The source gives three reasons tokens beat the alternatives: they let a model recognize meaningful word pieces (splitting "cooking" into "cook" + "ing"), they keep the vocabulary smaller than using whole words would (more efficient), and they help a model make sense of made-up or unfamiliar words by breaking them into familiar pieces (a nonsense word like "chatgpting" still gets some traction from "chatgpt" + "ing").

3. Self-supervision is the unlock: the data teaches itself

Before this, training a model meant supervision: paying people to label examples (this photo is a "cat", this transaction is "fraud"). That's expensive and it caps out. The source uses ImageNet as the example: at 5 cents per label, a million images cost $50,000 to label, and scaling to a million categories would run to roughly $50 million.

Self-supervision sidesteps this because, for a language model, you don't need anyone to write down the "right answer": the next word in a real sentence already is the right answer. Take any ordinary sentence you already have lying around and you can turn it into a whole stack of training examples for free, just by asking "given everything so far, what word comes next?" at every point in the sentence.

A sentence I made up to illustrate the mechanic described in the source (a 6-word sentence there yields six training pairs the same way). No labeling budget required, just text.

Because ordinary text is everywhere (books, articles, forum posts), this made it possible to build training sets vastly bigger than any hand-labeled dataset ever could be, and that's specifically what let language models scale up into what people now call LLMs. Worth flagging: self-supervision isn't the same as having no labels at all (that would be unsupervised learning). Here, labels exist, they're just extracted automatically from the data itself rather than written by a person.

One honest caveat from the source itself: "large language model" isn't a precise technical term. What counted as large kept shifting: 117 million parameters was a big deal in 2018 (GPT-1), 1.5 billion was the new bar a year later (GPT-2), and by the time this material was written, the bar had moved to around 100 billion. Treat any specific parameter-count threshold as a snapshot in time, not a fixed rule.

4. From language-only to "foundation": multimodality and general-purpose behavior

Once models started handling more than text (images, and eventually other data types), the term "large language model" stopped fitting well, since models like GPT-4V and Gemini clearly aren't just about language anymore. The source uses foundation model to cover both large language models and large multimodal models (LMMs, meaning generative models that work across modalities). A model that works with more than one modality at all is simply called multimodal; a generative one is an LMM.

The concrete example given is CLIP (OpenAI, 2021), which wasn't trained to generate text, it's an embedding model, trained to place matching images and captions near each other in a shared vector space. CLIP's trick was natural language supervision: instead of paying for labels, OpenAI collected 400 million (image, text) pairs that already existed together on the internet, roughly 400 times the size of the hand-labeled ImageNet dataset, again without manual labeling cost. That's self-supervision's core idea applied to a second modality.

The other half of what "foundation" signals is a move away from narrow, task-specific models (a model trained only for sentiment analysis couldn't also do translation) toward general-purpose ones that are usable, out of the box, across a wide range of tasks. You can then sharpen a general-purpose model toward your specific need using prompt engineering, RAG, or finetuning, techniques this material names here and says will be covered in depth later.

5. Why "AI engineering" is exploding as its own discipline right now

Building applications on top of ML models isn't new; recommendation systems, fraud detection, and churn prediction all predate this wave. What changed is that foundation models made two things true at once: AI got dramatically more capable, and the barrier to using it dropped sharply, because you no longer have to train a model yourself, you can call one through an API (the model as a service pattern). The source lays out three factors it says are together driving the growth of AI engineering as a field:

  1. General-purpose capability. Because a foundation model can do many tasks reasonably well out of the box, it opens up applications that weren't feasible before, which widens both the user base and the demand for AI-powered products.
  2. Rising investment. The source cites a Goldman Sachs Research estimate of AI investment approaching $100 billion in the US and $200 billion globally by 2025, and notes that (per FactSet) roughly one in three S&P 500 companies mentioned AI on their Q2 2023 earnings calls, three times the rate a year earlier. It flags this as correlation, not necessarily causation: it's unclear whether mentioning AI made companies more successful, or successful companies were just quicker to adopt new technology.
  3. Low barrier to entry. Model-as-a-service APIs mean you don't need infrastructure to host a model, and you can often build and iterate using plain English instead of writing lower-level code.

On the naming itself: the source explains it chose "AI engineering" over "ML engineering" (because working with foundation models differs enough from traditional ML work to need its own name) and over the various "-Ops" terms (because the emphasis here is more on adapting/engineering the model's behavior than on operations). It also mentions surveying 20 practitioners about what term they'd use, and most preferred "AI engineering".

6. What people are actually building: eight recurring use-case patterns

Rather than trying to list every possible AI application (which the source calls basically impossible, "there's probably an AI for that" for almost anything), it groups what it found (from interviewing companies, reading case studies, and studying 205 open-source AI projects with 500+ GitHub stars) into eight recurring categories. A single app often spans more than one category.

  • Coding. Consistently the most popular use case in surveys. GitHub Copilot is called out as one of the earliest production successes, crossing $100 million in annual recurring revenue about two years after launch. Coding-specific tools mentioned include ones for extracting structured data from PDFs, converting English to code, turning a screenshot or design into working code, translating between languages/frameworks, writing documentation, generating tests, and writing commit messages.
  • Image and video production. Cited as one of the strongest startup categories: Midjourney reportedly reached $200 million in annual recurring revenue at about a year and a half old (as of late 2023). The source also notes a shift in social norms around AI images, contrasting Facebook banning AI-generated profile photos in 2019 with many platforms building the same feature in by 2023.
  • Writing. The source cites an MIT study (Noy and Zhang, 2023) where 453 college-educated professionals given occupation-specific writing tasks, half with ChatGPT access, showed the ChatGPT group finishing 40% faster with output quality up 18%, and notes the gains were larger for workers who started out weaker at writing. It also flags a real downside: it reports the New York Times found Amazon flooded with shoddy AI-generated travel guidebooks in 2023, complete with fabricated author bios.
  • Education. Framed as double-edged: several US school districts banned ChatGPT over cheating concerns, then reversed course months later. Duolingo research (Pajak and Bicknell, 2022) is cited finding that of four stages of course creation, lesson personalization benefits most from AI. On the "disruption" side, the source notes Chegg's stock price falling from about $28 (when ChatGPT launched, November 2022) to about $2 by September 2024.
  • Conversational bots. Covers general chatbots, companion/therapist-style bots, customer support bots, and product copilots that walk users through complex tasks like insurance claims. The source also mentions AI-driven non-player characters (NPCs) in games as an emerging use of the same underlying idea.
  • Information aggregation. Summarizing and distilling information, e.g. "talk to your docs" tools for contracts and papers. A concrete example given: Instacart's internal prompt marketplace found its most popular template asked AI to summarize meeting notes, emails, and Slack messages into facts, open questions, and action items.
  • Data organization. Making unstructured data (photos, PDFs, logs, contracts) searchable and structured, e.g. Google Photos' AI-powered search. The source cites an estimate that the intelligent document processing (IDP) industry will reach $12.81 billion by 2030, growing about 32.9% a year, though as with most market-size forecasts, treat the precision of that figure with some skepticism.
  • Workflow automation. Automating repetitive tasks (booking, invoicing, data entry). This is where the source briefly introduces agents: AIs that can plan and use external tools (like a search engine or a calendar) to actually complete a task rather than just describing what to do. It flags agents as a topic covered in full later, and notes the current level of hype "borders on obsession" while still calling it not entirely unwarranted.
A methodology caveat worth carrying forward: this use-case picture leans on 205 open-source repositories with at least 500 GitHub stars, which the source itself says under-represents categories like education and data organization, since a lot of that work happens in closed enterprise tools that never show up on GitHub. Treat the category proportions as a rough sketch, not a precise market map.

Why it matters

Once you see that "foundation model" just means "trained cheaply and broadly enough, via next-token prediction on huge amounts of unlabeled text, to be generally useful," a lot of the surrounding hype gets easier to reason about. It explains why so many wildly different products (a coding assistant, a customer support bot, a photo editor) can all be built on the same handful of underlying models: they're not specialized tools, they're general capability that gets pointed at a task afterward, using prompting, retrieval, or finetuning. It also explains why "AI engineer" became a distinct role from "ML engineer" so fast: most of the day-to-day work shifted from training models to adapting and evaluating already-powerful ones, which is a genuinely different skill set even though a lot of the underlying engineering discipline (metrics, experimentation, feedback loops) carries over unchanged.

Remember this

  • A "foundation model" is a large, general-purpose model, trained via next-token prediction (or the multimodal equivalent) on huge amounts of text or other data, that's broad enough to be adapted to many different tasks rather than built for just one.
  • Self-supervision is the whole trick: the "label" for training is just the next word already sitting in the text, so no human has to write it down. That's what let training data scale from thousands of hand-labeled examples to essentially the whole internet.
  • Autoregressive models (predict what's next, using only the past) power almost all of today's generation; masked models (fill in a gap using both sides) are better suited to understanding tasks.
  • AI engineering exists as a distinct discipline because foundation models made building on top of AI both more capable and much cheaper to start, largely thanks to the model-as-a-service pattern (call an API instead of training your own model).
  • Real AI applications tend to fall into a small number of recurring shapes (coding, image/video, writing, education, conversational bots, information aggregation, data organization, workflow automation), and most useful products actually straddle more than one of these.

Try it yourself

  • Hand-simulate self-supervision. Write one original sentence. On paper, manually create every (context → next word) training pair from it, the way the schematic above does. Notice you never had to write an "answer key" separately, it was already there.
  • Tokenize three ways. Take a sentence and split it into (a) individual characters, (b) whole words, and (c) your own guessed subword chunks (roots + endings, like "learn" + "ing"). Count the units each way and think about why tokens land in between characters and words as a compromise.
  • Completion vs. conversation test. Using any chatbot you already have access to, give it a plain unfinished sentence (a completion-style prompt) versus a direct question. Compare how differently it responds, and notice that "having a conversation" isn't automatic just from being good at completion.
  • Run your own AI-exposure audit. List 5 tasks from your week. For each, ask the source's own bar: could AI plausibly cut the time needed by at least half today? Tally how "exposed" your own work actually is, honestly.
  • Map your own AI usage. For one day, note every AI tool you touch and sort each into one of the eight use-case buckets above. See which categories actually show up in your life versus which ones you mostly just hear about.
  • Try a multimodal prompt. Using a chat app that accepts both image and text input, upload a photo and ask a question about it, then ask a text-only follow-up in the same conversation. Notice how the model is conditioning its next-token predictions on both modalities at once.
