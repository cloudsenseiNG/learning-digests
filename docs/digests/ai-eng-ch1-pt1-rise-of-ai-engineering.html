AI ENGINEERING · CH.1 DIGEST (PART 1)

The Rise of AI Engineering

How a next-word-guessing machine turned into the fastest-growing engineering discipline around, and what "foundation model" actually means once you unpack it.

Part 1 of Chapter 1. This chapter is long, so we're covering it in a couple of passes: today is "The Rise of AI Engineering" (from language models to self-supervision to foundation models to why AI engineering exists as its own job). Use cases, planning an AI product, and the AI stack are coming in a later digest.

The big idea

Everything people call "AI" right now, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, traces back to one surprisingly simple trick: a model that guesses the next word. What changed in the last few years isn't that this trick got invented, it's that it got trained on a scale nobody had bothered with before, and that scale only became affordable because of a specific training shortcut called self-supervision. That shortcut is the whole reason "AI engineering" (building on top of these models) is now a distinct job from traditional ML engineering (building the models yourself).

Key terms

  • Token: the basic unit a language model reads and writes. Not always a whole word: it can be a character, a full word, or a word fragment (like "-tion").
  • Tokenization: the process of chopping text into tokens before a model can touch it. The set of tokens a model knows is its vocabulary.
  • Autoregressive model: predicts the next token using only what came before it. This is what ChatGPT-style models are.
  • Masked language model: predicts a missing token using context from both sides (fill-in-the-blank). BERT is the classic example.
  • Supervision: training a model on data that a human has labeled (e.g. "fraud" / "not fraud").
  • Self-supervision: training where the labels come for free from the data itself, no human labeling needed. This is the unlock that let language models scale up.
  • Foundation model: a large, general-purpose model (trained via self-supervision at scale) that can be adapted to many different tasks, instead of being built for just one.
  • Multimodal model: a model that works with more than one kind of data (text and images, for instance), not just text.

How it works

Language models are completion machines

At its core, a language model encodes the statistics of a language: given some text, how likely is each possible next chunk of text? The chapter's example: given "My favorite color is __", a decent English model should predict "blue" far more often than "car". That's it, that's the whole trick. Feed it a prompt, it predicts what plausibly comes next.

Models don't read whole words, they read tokens. GPT-4 reportedly breaks "I can't wait to build AI applications" into nine tokens, splitting "can't" into "can" and "'t". An average token runs about three-quarters the length of a word, so roughly 100 tokens ≈ 75 words. Model vocabularies (the full set of tokens a model knows) vary: the chapter cites Mixtral 8x7B at 32,000 tokens and GPT-4 at 100,256. Tokens exist as a middle ground: fewer distinct units than whole words (which keeps the vocabulary manageable) but more meaning-carrying than single characters (splitting "cooking" into "cook" + "ing" keeps both pieces meaningful, and lets the model handle made-up words like "chatgpting" by recognizing the pieces).

There are two flavors of language model, and they differ in which direction they're allowed to look:

  • Masked models fill in a blank using context from before and after it. Good for tasks like classifying sentiment or debugging code, where understanding the whole surrounding context matters more than generating new text.
  • Autoregressive models only look backward, predicting the next token from everything said so far, then repeating that to generate token after token. This is what makes them generative, and it's why they're the default choice for anything that writes text, and (per the book) why the term "language model" in the rest of that text means autoregressive unless stated otherwise.
The direction a model is allowed to "look" decides whether it's built for generating text or for understanding it.

Self-supervision: the unlock that made scale affordable

Most ML models before this wave needed supervision, meaning a human labels a bunch of examples first (this transaction is "fraud", this image is a "balloon"). That's expensive. The chapter's numbers: labeling one image at 5 cents each means $50,000 to label a million images for something like ImageNet, and scaling categories up to a million would push labeling alone toward $50 million.

Simplification: the book also cites an actual 2024 AWS SageMaker Ground Truth price point (8 cents per image under 50,000 images, 2 cents per image over 1 million) as an illustration of real labeling costs. Treat that specific number as a snapshot from when the book was written, not a live price, since cloud pricing changes.

Language models sidestep this entirely through self-supervision: the text itself already contains both the "question" and the "answer". Take a sentence you've never seen labeled anywhere, say, "AI ate my homework." Just by sliding a window across it, you get a free training example at every step: given "AI", predict "ate"; given "AI ate", predict "my"; given "AI ate my", predict "homework"; and so on. Nobody labeled any of that, the sentence itself did. Because raw text is everywhere (books, articles, comments), this made it possible to build training sets orders of magnitude larger than any hand-labeled dataset, which is the entire reason "large" became possible in "large language model."

Self-supervision: no human labeled any of the "correct answers" above, the sentence supplied its own.

Model size is usually measured in parameters (the internal variables the training process adjusts). The chapter tracks how fast "large" has moved: OpenAI's first GPT had 117 million parameters in June 2018 and was considered large; GPT-2 arrived in February 2019 with 1.5 billion and made 117 million look small; by the time the book was written, 100 billion parameters was the bar for "large." The lesson isn't the specific numbers, it's that "large" is a moving target that keeps resetting every year or two.

From language models to foundation models

Text-only models are limited, since humans take in the world through vision and sound too, not just language. So language models have been extended to handle other data types: GPT-4V and Claude 3 can read images alongside text, and some models handle video, 3D data, and more. A model that handles more than one data type is multimodal; a generative multimodal model is sometimes called a large multimodal model (LMM).

This is the point where the book stops calling these things "large language models" and starts calling them foundation models: the word signals both how central they've become and that they're meant to be built upon for many different needs, not just one. That's also a shift from task-specific models (a sentiment model that can only do sentiment) to general-purpose ones (one model that can do sentiment analysis and translation and more, out of the box). The book names three common ways to adapt a general-purpose foundation model to a specific job: prompt engineering (crafting detailed instructions), RAG, or retrieval-augmented generation (connecting the model to an external database it can pull from), and finetuning (further training the model on your own data). All three get their own dedicated chapters later in the book.

Why "AI engineering" is a new job title

People have built applications on top of ML models for over a decade, usually called ML engineering or MLOps. The book argues AI engineering earns its own name because of three factors arriving together:

  • General-purpose capability: one foundation model can now do many jobs a specialized model used to be built for, which massively widens who can use AI and for what.
  • Investment: money followed the capability jump. The book cites a Goldman Sachs Research estimate of AI investment approaching $100 billion in the US and $200 billion globally by 2025, and a FactSet finding that roughly one in three S&P 500 companies mentioned AI on their Q2 2023 earnings calls, three times the year before.
  • Lower barrier to entry: the "model as a service" approach (call a hosted model over an API instead of training and hosting your own) means you don't need the infrastructure or the specialized talent that building a foundation model requires. You can build an app on top of one with an API key and, increasingly, plain English instructions instead of code.

Simplification: the specific growth stats in this section (GitHub star counts for AI tooling passing Bitcoin's, a LinkedIn survey showing "Generative AI" skill mentions rising, the Goldman Sachs and FactSet figures) are all snapshots the book reports as of its writing. They're useful for the shape of the trend, not as current numbers, treat them as "this is roughly how fast this was moving at the time," not live data.

Why it matters

Understanding that foundation models are fundamentally next-token predictors, trained cheaply at scale via self-supervision, explains a lot of what you'll run into later: why they're confidently wrong sometimes (they're predicting plausible text, not looking anything up), why prompt engineering and RAG and finetuning are the three main levers you have (you're adapting a general model, not building one), and why this field is moving so fast (the barrier to entry collapsed for basically everyone at once).

Remember this

  • A language model is a completion machine: give it text, it predicts plausible next tokens. That's the whole primitive everything else is built on.
  • Self-supervision is why scale became affordable: the training labels come free from the text itself, no human labeling required.
  • "Foundation model" means large, general-purpose, and adaptable, not just "big language model." Multimodal models (text + image, etc.) count too.
  • Prompt engineering, RAG, and finetuning are the three standard ways to adapt a general foundation model to your specific task.
  • AI engineering exists as separate from ML engineering because you're now adapting existing models via APIs, not training your own from scratch.

Try it yourself

  • Build a naive tokenizer. Write a script that splits any paragraph on whitespace and punctuation, then count the unique tokens (your naive vocabulary size). Compare it by eye to how a real tokenizer might split the same text (e.g. paste it into any tokenizer visualizer you have access to) to see subword splitting in action.
  • Self-supervision generator. Take any sentence, write a script that automatically produces every "context → next token" training pair from it (like the worked example above), and print them out. No labeling, all mechanical, that's the whole trick made visible.
  • Vocabulary size explorer. Take a text file of any length you have lying around, count unique whole words versus unique characters. Notice how far apart those two numbers are, that gap is exactly the space tokens are designed to sit in.
  • Labeling cost calculator. Write a tiny script that takes a number of examples and a cost per label, and computes total labeling cost. Plug in the chapter's ImageNet numbers (1 million images, 5 cents each) and confirm you land on its $50,000 estimate.
  • Parameter growth timeline. Plot (a spreadsheet chart is fine) the parameter counts and dates from this digest: GPT at 117 million in 2018, GPT-2 at 1.5 billion in 2019, "100 billion considered large" as of the book's writing. Eyeball how steep that curve is.
  • Completion machine demo. If you have access to any text generation tool, feed it a half-finished sentence with no instructions attached, just the raw text, and see it complete it rather than "answer" it. That's the raw completion behavior the chapter describes before instruction-following gets layered on top.
