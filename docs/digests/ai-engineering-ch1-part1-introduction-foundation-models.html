From Language Models to Foundation Models, and What People Are Already Building With Them

The idea that finally made "foundation model" click for me: it's basically a next-word guesser that got so big, and learned so cheaply, that guessing well turned out to be enough for a whole lot of real products.

Part 1 of 2 for this chapter. Covers "The Rise of AI Engineering" and "Foundation Model Use Cases" (pp. 25–51 of the PDF). Part 2 will cover "Planning AI Applications" and "The AI Engineering Stack" (pp. 52–72).

In one line

Foundation models are large, general-purpose prediction machines that learned to write (and see, and hear) by training on huge amounts of ordinary text and media, without anyone hand-labeling it, and that shift is what turned "building an AI application" from a rare, expensive research project into something almost anyone can do.

The big idea

For a long time, if you wanted an AI model to do something, you had to build and train that model yourself, which meant collecting a labeled dataset, which is slow and expensive. Language models found a shortcut: because ordinary text already contains its own "right answers" (the next word in a sentence), a model can generate millions of training examples out of text nobody labeled for it. Scale that up with enormous amounts of internet text, and you get models capable enough to be useful for many different tasks out of the box, not just one. That shift, from "train your own narrow model" to "adapt a model someone else already trained," is what this chapter calls foundation models, and building applications on top of them is what the book calls AI engineering.

Key terms

  • Language model — a model that has learned the statistical patterns of a language, so it can estimate how likely a word is to come next given some text. Example: given "My favorite color is __", it should rate "blue" as more likely than "car".
  • Token — the basic unit a language model reads and writes. Can be a whole word, part of a word, or a character, depending on the model. As a rough rule of thumb, 100 tokens is roughly 75 words for GPT-4 style tokenizers.
  • Tokenization — the process of breaking raw text into tokens before a model can process it.
  • Vocabulary — the fixed set of tokens a model knows. GPT-4's vocabulary has 100,256 tokens; Mixtral 8x7B's has 32,000. Bigger vocabularies aren't automatically better, they're a design choice.
  • Masked language model — trained to fill in a blank using context from both before and after it (like a fill-in-the-blank quiz). BERT is the well-known example. Common for classification-style tasks, not for generating long text.
  • Autoregressive language model — trained to predict only the next token, using just what came before. This is what GPT-style models do, one token at a time, and it's the type most people mean when they say "language model" today.
  • Supervision (supervised learning) — training a model on data that a human has labeled first, e.g. transactions marked "fraud" or "not fraud".
  • Self-supervision — training where the labels are pulled automatically out of the input data itself, no human labeling needed. This is how language models are trained (see "How it works" below). Different from unsupervised learning, where there's no label at all, even an inferred one.
  • Parameter — a number inside a model that gets adjusted during training. Loosely, "how many parameters" is a proxy for a model's size and learning capacity, though the definition of "large" keeps moving: 117 million parameters was considered large for GPT in 2018; by the time this book was written, 100 billion was the bar.
  • Foundation model — a large model, trained on broad data (often more than one modality, like text and images), general-purpose enough to be adapted to many different downstream tasks rather than built for just one.
  • Multimodal model — a model that can take in or generate more than one type of data (text, images, audio, etc). A generative multimodal model is also called a large multimodal model (LMM).
  • Embedding model — a model trained to turn data (text, an image) into a vector of numbers that captures its meaning, so similar things end up with similar vectors. Not generative. CLIP, mentioned below, is an example.
  • Model as a service — the setup where a company trains an expensive foundation model and then rents access to it through an API, so you don't have to train or host anything yourself.
  • AI engineering — the discipline of building applications on top of an existing foundation model, as opposed to building the model itself.
  • Prompt engineering / RAG / finetuning — the three main ways to adapt a general-purpose foundation model to your specific task, introduced briefly here and covered in depth later in the book. Prompt engineering means crafting the instructions/context you give the model; RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) means connecting the model to an external database it can pull facts from; finetuning means further training the model's own weights on your data.

How it works

Grounding paragraph. Before this chapter, when people said "AI," they often meant a model built for one specific job: a fraud detector, a recommender system, an image classifier. Those models still exist and still matter. What's different since around 2020 is scale: models trained on far more data and far more compute than before became capable of many different jobs at once, using the same underlying model. To understand why that happened, and why it happened now, you need to see how language models are trained, why that training method is so cheap to scale, and how it grew from "predict the next word" into something that can see images, follow instructions, and get adapted into products. That's the thread this section follows.

1. A language model is a next-token guesser

State it simply: a language model reads some text and predicts what token is likely to come next, over and over. It doesn't "know" facts the way a database does; it has learned statistical patterns about which tokens tend to follow which other tokens.

Concrete example: feed an autoregressive model the prompt "To be or not to be" and it might complete it with ", that is the question." It isn't retrieving a memorized quote from a lookup table (though it may have seen it during training); it's predicting, token by token, what's statistically likely to follow. Because these outputs are open-ended rather than picked from a fixed list, this kind of model is called generative, which is where the term "generative AI" comes from.

Why it matters: a huge number of practical tasks can be reframed as "complete this text." Translation can be framed as completing "How are you in French is …". Spam detection can be framed as completing "Is this email spam? Answer: …". Once you see tasks this way, a single next-token predictor can, in principle, do a lot more than write essays.

2. Two ways to predict: masked vs. autoregressive

State it simply: language models split into two families based on what context they're allowed to use when predicting a token. A masked model can look both backward and forward around a gap; an autoregressive model can only look backward, at what's already been generated.

Concrete example: given "My favorite ___ is blue", a masked model (like BERT) predicts the blank using words on both sides, and would likely guess "color." Given "My favorite color is ___", an autoregressive model (like GPT) can only use the words before the blank, and predicts what comes next, one token at a time, continuing to feed its own output back in as new input.

Why it matters: because it can only see what came before, an autoregressive model can keep generating token after token indefinitely, which is exactly what you need for open-ended text generation. That's why autoregressive models are the default for chatbots and writing tools today, while masked models are more commonly used for understanding-only tasks like classification, where you're not generating new text at all.

3. Self-supervision: how a model gets "free" training data

State it simply: instead of paying humans to label examples, a language model turns raw text into its own training set. Every sentence already contains the "correct answer" for what should come after each point in it.

Why it matters: labeling data by hand is slow and expensive. The book gives a concrete sense of scale: labeling a dataset like ImageNet (a bit over 1 million images) at around 5 cents an image comes out to roughly $50,000, and scaling that same approach up to a million categories instead of 1,000 would push the labeling cost alone toward $50 million. Self-supervision sidesteps this entirely for text, because the "labels" are just the next words in text that already exists, in books, articles, and web pages. That's the real unlock: it's what let language models train on enormous amounts of internet-scale text without a human labeling budget, which is what let them scale up into large language models (LLMs) in the first place. Worth flagging: self-supervision is not the same as unsupervised learning. Self-supervision still produces labels, it just infers them automatically from the input instead of a human providing them; unsupervised learning has no labels at all.

4. From text-only LLMs to multimodal foundation models

State it simply: once language models started incorporating other kinds of data, not just text, they outgrew the name "large language model" and became known as foundation models, a term meant to capture that they're general enough to be built upon for many different applications.

Concrete example: GPT-4V and Claude 3 can take in both text and images. To train this kind of capability without manual labeling, OpenAI used a technique called natural language supervision to build CLIP, a model that learned from image-text pairs that already co-occurred on the internet (a photo and its caption, for instance) rather than pairs a human deliberately labeled. That gave them roughly 400 million (image, text) pairs, about 400 times the size of ImageNet, without paying for manual labels. It's worth noting CLIP itself isn't generative, it's an embedding model: it learned to represent images and text as vectors capturing meaning, not to generate new text or images. Embedding models like CLIP became the backbone that later generative multimodal models (like Flamingo and LLaVA) are built on top of.

Why it matters: a model that only ever worked with text couldn't, say, look at a photo and describe it, or read a chart. Multimodality is part of what makes today's foundation models feel closer to how a person perceives the world, through more than one channel at once. It's also part of what "general-purpose" means here: the same base model, once it can handle multiple modalities and a wide range of tasks, doesn't need to be rebuilt from scratch for every new job the way older, task-specific models did (a sentiment-analysis model couldn't also do translation; a foundation model can attempt both out of the box, then be adapted further with prompt engineering, RAG, or finetuning to do either one better).

5. Why "AI engineering" exists as a discipline now

State it simply: three forces, arriving together, made it possible for far more people to build AI applications without training a model themselves: foundation models can do many tasks well out of the box, investment poured in once that became obvious, and the "model as a service" API approach removed the need to host anything yourself.

Concrete example: the book points to the growth of open source AI tooling as one signal of this: within about two years, several AI engineering tools had already collected more GitHub stars than Bitcoin, on a trajectory to pass even established web frameworks. On the investment side, Goldman Sachs Research estimated AI investment could approach $100 billion in the US and $200 billion globally by 2025, and roughly one in three S&P 500 companies mentioned AI on their Q2 2023 earnings calls, three times the rate from a year before (the book is careful to note it's unclear whether mentioning AI causes better stock performance, or whether already-successful companies are just quicker to adopt new technology, so treat that correlation cautiously).

Why it matters: this is the honest answer to "why now, and not ten years ago." People have been building applications on top of ML models for well over a decade already (fraud detection, recommender systems, churn prediction). What changed is that you no longer need to train a model to get started, you can rent access to an already-capable one through an API and start adapting it to your problem the same weekend. That lower barrier is also exactly why the book spends its later chapters on the buy-versus-build and defensibility questions that come with it (previewed in Part 2 of this digest).

6. What people are actually building with this

State it simply: the book groups the flood of foundation-model applications it studied (50 companies interviewed, 100+ case studies read, 205 open source projects with 500+ GitHub stars examined) into eight overlapping categories. A single application can span more than one.

  • Coding. The most popular use case in survey after survey. GitHub Copilot, a code-completion tool, crossed $100 million in annual recurring revenue only two years after launch. Beyond general coding help, tools specialize in narrower jobs: pulling structured data out of PDFs, translating between programming languages, writing docs, generating commit messages. McKinsey researchers found AI can roughly double developer productivity on documentation and lift it 25-50% for code generation and refactoring, with much smaller gains on highly complex tasks.
  • Image and video production. Midjourney (image generation) reportedly reached $200 million in annual recurring revenue at just a year and a half old. AI is used for ad variations (like adding seasonal snow or changing leaf colors), photo editing, and profile pictures, a use case that was actually banned by Facebook in 2019 for safety reasons and is now mainstream.
  • Writing. An MIT study (Noy and Zhang, 2023) had 453 college-educated professionals do occupation-specific writing tasks, half with ChatGPT access. Those with access finished 40% faster with output quality rated 18% higher, and the gap narrowed most for people who weren't naturally strong writers. On the downside, the same capability enabled content farms: NewsGuard identified nearly 400 ads from 141 known brands running on junk AI-generated websites in June 2023, one of which was publishing 1,200 articles a day.
  • Education. AI can summarize textbooks, generate personalized lesson plans, and act as a tutor or debate partner. Duolingo researchers (Pajak and Bicknell, 2022) found AI helps most at the lesson-personalization stage of course creation. The flip side: Chegg, a homework-help company, saw its share price fall from $28 when ChatGPT launched in November 2022 to $2 by September 2024, as students shifted to asking AI directly.
  • Conversational bots. Customer support bots, product copilots, companion/therapist-style bots, and smart NPCs in games (which previously ran on limited scripted dialogue). Enterprises favor these because they can respond faster than a human agent can.
  • Information aggregation. Summarizing documents, "talk to your docs" tools, meeting-note digestion. Instacart's internal prompt marketplace found its most popular template was one that summarized meetings and emails into facts, open questions, and action items automatically routed to a project tracker.
  • Data organization. Turning unstructured data (photos, PDFs, contracts) into something searchable: think Google Photos surfacing images by description, or extracting fields from a receipt or driver's license automatically. The intelligent document processing (IDP) market was estimated to reach $12.81 billion by 2030, growing roughly 33% a year.
  • Workflow automation. Booking, form-filling, lead management, invoicing, and beyond, especially once a model gets access to external tools (a search engine, a calendar, a phone line) rather than just generating text. Systems that can plan and use tools like this are called agents, a topic the book returns to later.

Why it matters: notice how many of these examples aren't just "cool demo," they're companies with real, dated, verifiable revenue or market impact. That's the honest signal that this isn't hype without substance, even though (as you'll see in Part 2 of this digest) a good demo and a good product are two very different bars to clear.

Why it matters

This chapter is the "why now" chapter. If you're picking up AI engineering in 2026, it's easy to take foundation models for granted, but the mechanics matter: self-supervision is the specific reason a next-word predictor could be trained on something as vast and messy as the internet without a human labeling budget, and that's the reason it got good enough, and general enough, to be useful for coding, writing, image generation, tutoring, and dozens of other jobs from a single underlying model. Once you understand that a foundation model is fundamentally a very capable prediction engine, not a hand-built expert system, a lot of what comes later in this book (why prompting matters, why evaluation is hard, why hallucination happens) stops feeling mysterious and starts feeling like a natural consequence of how the thing was trained.

Remember this
  • A language model is a next-token (or next-word) predictor. Everything else, chatbots, coding assistants, translators, is that same mechanic pointed at different prompts.
  • Self-supervision is the unlock: ordinary text already contains its own training labels, so language models could scale on huge amounts of data without expensive human labeling, which supervised approaches (like ImageNet-style image classification) can't avoid.
  • "Foundation model" means general-purpose and multimodal-capable, not just "a big language model." It marks a shift from building one model per task to adapting one broad model to many tasks.
  • AI engineering exists as a distinct discipline because you can now build on top of someone else's trained model via an API (model as a service), instead of training your own, which is what makes it faster and cheaper to start than traditional ML engineering.
  • The use cases already generating real revenue (Copilot, Midjourney) are grounded in the same mechanic: a general-purpose predictor, adapted to a specific job.

Try it yourself

Try it yourself
  • Tokenize your own sentence. Use OpenAI's public tokenizer tool (or the tiktoken Python library) on a sentence you write. Count tokens vs. words and see how close it lands to the "100 tokens ≈ 75 words" rule of thumb from this chapter. (~30 min)
  • Build a tiny next-word predictor. Take a short piece of text (a paragraph or two), write a script that counts, for every pair of consecutive words, how often each word follows the one before it (a bigram frequency table). Given a starting word, have your script pick the most frequent next word, repeatedly, and see what it generates. This is a toy, non-neural version of what an autoregressive model does. (~1-2 hrs, any language)
  • Recreate the self-supervision table by hand. Pick any sentence, write out every (context → next token) pair it produces, the way the schematic above does. Do it for three different sentences and notice how a single paragraph could generate dozens of training examples for free. (~30-45 min)
  • Compare masked vs. autoregressive on the same sentence. Try a fill-mask demo (e.g. a Hugging Face BERT "fill-mask" pipeline, or an equivalent free playground) and a chat/completion model on the same sentence with a blank at the end vs. in the middle. Notice the different behavior. (~1 hr)
  • Estimate a labeling budget. Write a tiny script or spreadsheet: given a per-item labeling cost and a dataset size, calculate total labeling cost. Reproduce the book's ImageNet math ($0.05/image, ~1 million images) and then try it at 1 million categories instead of 1,000. Compare that number to "free" (self-supervised text). (~30 min)
  • Categorize five apps you already use. Pick five apps with AI features you use regularly and sort each into one (or more) of the eight use-case categories from this chapter (coding, image/video, writing, education, conversational bots, info aggregation, data organization, workflow automation). Note which categories are missing from your daily life entirely, that's a hint at where the "boring but valuable" opportunities might be. (~30-45 min)
